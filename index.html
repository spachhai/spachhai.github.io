I am a postdoctoral research fellow at the Department of Geology and Geophysics, University of Utah. My research focuses on the quantitative study of Earth structure and dynamic processes using geophysical observations and the development of computational and statistical methods including machine learning algorithm to elucidate Earth structure with rigorous uncertainties. It is challenging to compute waveforms for realistic three-dimensional Earth structure that includes small-scale structures in the deep Earth interior due to the computational cost it demands. Some of the machine learning algorithms may help in addressing this challenge.

Research Statement

The analysis of seismological observations (e.g., seismic waves, Earth normal modes – the vibrational pattern due to large earthquakes, Movie 1) is a key component to inferring the structure and dynamic processes of the Earth. My research interests include the quantitative study of Earth structure and dynamic processes by inversion of seismological observations and the development of computational methods to elucidate Earth structure. My past and current studies focus on developement and implementation of probabilistic (Bayesian) hierarchical inversion to achieve better understanding of the structure, composition, and dynamics of the solid Earth interior. While increased data quality, new inference techniques, and increased computational power have improved our understanding of the Earth’s interior, the interpretation of inversion results remains challenging because of inherent nonuniqueness (many models explain the data equally well) and nonlinearities. I assert that rigorous model parameter estimates and uncertainties are required for meaningful physical interpretation. In Bayesian inversion, parameters are considered as random variables and their values are represented by probability densities that express degrees of belief. Parameters are assigned prior information which is independent of the data and updated by data information to produce the inversion result (posterior probability density) from which rigorous parameter values and uncertainties can be inferred. In addition to Earth structure, we can treat noise in the data as unknown and infer them applying hierarchical Bayesian approach.

Movie 1: (Left) Compressional(P) and shear(S) waves passing through the Earth interior (source: IRIS (Middle) 0S2 and (Right) 0S4 Earth's normal mode animation; source https://saviot.cnrs.fr/terre/index.en.html )

Core-reflected waves (i.e., PcP and ScP, Figure 1) have been extensively utilized to constrain small-scale structures that exhibit a strong decrease in shear (S)- and compressional (P)- wave velocity above the CMB, known as Ultra Low Velocity Zones (ULVZs). However, the details of extent and fine structure are poorly understood because of the nonuniqueness (Figure 2) and nonlinearity of the inverse problem. Therefore, I developed a novel Bayesian approach to constrain ULVZ parameter values and uncertainties. I applied this method to ScP waves recorded by the Hi-Net array (Japan) that sample the CMB beneath the east of Philippines. This study found a complex ULVZ with dense material sitting at the bottom of two layers. An extension of this study generalizes the inversion to consider an unknown model parameterization (i.e. the number of layers and data noise are unknown — a trans-dimensional model). Additionally, the noise in the data also treated as uknown extending the method to hierarchical trans-dimensional Bayesian inversion. The new study produces more general, but consistent, results than our previous work and, in particular, more meaningful uncertainties which improves comparisons between data sets. Recently, I also applied the trans-dimensional (trans-D) approach to additional waveforms recorded by the Australian transportable array WOMBAT to study an extensive CMB area beneath the east of Australia.

Another project improves the measurement of splitting functions (a visualization of the radial average of the normal-mode sensed structure, Figure 3) for inner core sensitive modes, which are relatively well isolated in frequency, by inversion of normal mode spectra (Figure 3). Splitting functions depend linearly on isotropic and anisotropic velocities in the Earth. Inner core structure has been extensively studied using normal modes and body waves but is still controversial because of strong non-linearity. Therefore, my project applies nonlinear optimization (neighborhood algorithm — NA) to compute the splitting functions of the 13S2 (spheroidal mode with radial order 13 and angular order 2) mode. For the observed data, the splitting functions of this mode are dominated by zonal structure with positive frequency anomalies near the poles and negative frequency anomalies along the equator. One of the main benefits of the NA approach is that the splitting functions of this mode can be constrained with significantly fewer events in comparison to those using traditional approaches. Limitations of the NA approach are under sampling for higher orders (billions of samples are required) and lack of rigorous uncertainty estimation. To address the limitations, I have adapted the Bayesian ULVZ inversion to this problem and successfully applied for 19 inner-core sensitive modes. I am currently working on the application of this approach to the mantle sensitive modes.

I am also working on the crustal and mantle structure using both radial and transverse components of receiver functions and surface wave dispersions, mainly focused on Himalaya, Afar, and Iran.

Current Projects

Project 1

Ultra-low Velocity Zone Structures (ULVZs) using ScP waveform inversion

I am continuously searching for ULVZ using ScP waveforms (shown in the previous section) sampling different locations of the core-mantle boundary. I am also interested in applying multi-disciplinary approach to understand the physical origin of ULVZs. For such a study, we recently applied geodynamical modeling of ULVZs we observed beneath the Coral Sea — east of Australia. In this study, we found that ULVZs are thin on two sides and occur at the edge of the Pacific large low-shear-velocity province. Our geodynamic modelling shows that ULVZs are compositional heterogeneous within them that may be caused by the remnants of Earth’s early differentiation. These structures are compositionally distinct from their surroundings and reside at the bottom of the mantle without full homogenization throughout Earth’s history.

This processes is summarized in Figure 1. In the initial molten stage of the magma ocean, dense materials sink downward due to gravitational instabilities15. The magma ocean starts to crystallize from the middle (Fig. 1A) and crystalizes faster towards the surface and slower towards the CMB due to different heat flux near the surface and the CMB (Fig. 1B). As the magma ocean crystalizes, melt is increasingly iron enriched, which in turn increases intrinsic density. As a result, a layer of intrinsically dense materials remains after the crystallization of the basal magma ocean, and the intrinsic density of this layer increases with depth (Fig. 1b). The global layer of intrinsically dense ULVZ materials on the CMB are later pushed by mantle flow into isolated ULVZ patches with variable and significant topography near the edges of large-low shear velocity provinces (Fig. 1c-d), and the depth-dependence of the intrinsic density anomaly of the ULVZs persist until the present.

The geodynamic modeling results are shown in movies below. Movie 1 (left column) shows a time series of the temperature field (bottom row), the residual buoyancy field (buoyancy field with the horizontal average removed; top row), and the chemical field (middle row). The ULVZ patches at the edges of the thermochemical pile have triangular shapes, and they are thicker in the middle and thinner at both flanks. In the middle of the ULVZ patches, the magnitude of the negative residual buoyancy increases with depth, while the residual buoyancy becomes more homogeneous towards the margins of the ULVZ patches. The increase of the density anomaly (or the magnitude of negative residual buoyancy) of the ULVZ patches with depth is more prominent during the early stages of the calculation, and this phenomenon gradually becomes weaker due to mixing. However, the density anomaly remains increasing with depth after 4.5 Gyr. Notably, the features of ULVZ patches in this geodynamic model are consistent with the observed ULVZs where density increases with depth and thickness decreases farther into the LLSVPs. The density increase, combined with the temperature increase as a function of ULVZ depth can explain the decrease of S-wave velocity with depth. Our modeling also explains the spatial variations of density and velocity within the ULVZs as well as a rapid spatial change of ULVZ thickness (Figure 1 above). To study this result further, two more experiments are performed, in which the initial density anomaly of the dense layer increases from 1.5% to 10% (case 2, middle column), and from 1.5% to 5% (case 3, right column) at the CMB. These results show that the density anomaly within the ULVZ patches homogenizes faster for lower initial density contrasts of the dense layer. In addition, a ULVZ with lower initial density material mixes faster into LLSVP materials

Currently I am applying this approach to other locations of the core-mantle boundary to better understand ULVZ origin. I am also further developing the hierarchical Bayesian inversion approach to simultaneously model the data from multiple earthquakes to better constrain the ULVZ parameters and remove some of the trade-off between ULVZ parameters.

Project 2

Global mapping of ULVZ-like structures using entropy

Although ULVZ structures are small, the study of their physical origin is likely essential for understanding the chemical and dynamical processes in the lowermost mantle, and their link to large-scale mantle structures. However, most of the core-mantle boundary region remains unexamined in terms of ULVZs, and their interpretation is generally based on ULVZs found in limited locations. This study analyzes global SKS – S wave passing through the outer core as P), and core-diffracted (Sdiff – S wave diffracted along the mantle side of the core-mantle boundary with epicentral distance > 100°. We characterize waveform complexity to identify possible ULVZ locations without quantifying ULVZ properties. quantifying ULVZ properties.

Here, we estimate waveform complexity by measuring the sample entropy of the time series at different decimating factors (defined as scale). For a fixed scale, waveforms with higher complexity produce a higher value of entropy, while signals with a higher degree of regularity (i.e. lower complexity) yield lower values of entropy. This approach has been applied in biological sciences to discriminate between states of health in individuals (e.g. Richman & Moorman, 2000; Costa & Healey, 2003; Costa et al., 2005; Humeau-Heurtier, 2015). Recently, it has also been applied to climate data (Balzter et al., 2015), but to our knowledge this approach has not yet been applied to seismic data. For more details we refer to Pachhai et al. (2022).

Project 3

Global mapping of ULVZ-like structures using SPdKS waveform complexity

Previous studies have examined nearly 17% area of the CMB out of which approximately 10% of the CMB locations contains ULVZs (Yu & Garnero, 2018). We have no knowledge of whether the remaining 83% of CMB area has ULVZs. To cover wider area of the core-mantle boundary, we considered a global dataset of SPdKS waveforms and analyzed a subset of these data in the distance range from 106 to 115 degree distance in search of highly anomalous SPdKS waveforms. Here we apply the Bayesian probalistic approach to identify the complexities in the SPdKS waveforms resulting from the complexities in the lowermost mantle. The probability map for the existance of ULVZ-like structures is shown in Figure 1. For more details, I refer to Thorne et al. (2020)

Project 4

Deep Earth structure using free-oscillation of the Earth excited by large earthquakes

Normal-mode structure coefficients are crucial observations to infer the velocity, density and attenuation structure of the deep Earth interior, but estimating these coefficients from Earth’s normal mode spectra is a non-linear inverse problem. Additionally, complete source information is typically unknown for large earthquakes, and there is a trade-off between the earthquake source and attenuation. Therefore, proper estimation of elastic and anelastic structure coefficients with their uncertainties becomes challenging. Here, we combine amatrix autoregression and a fully non-linear hierarchical Bayesian approach to address existing limitations. The hierarchical approach additionally quantifies the noise in the data. We applied this approach to the inner-core sensitive spheroidal (S) modes measured for earthquakes from 1994 to 2016. We further implement a Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) to assess whether anelastic structure can be estimated reliably well. Our model selection criterion indicates that anelastic structure coefficients are required only for modes with strong shear-wave energy in the inner core. Inversion results also show a strong correlation between elastic and anelastic splitting functions for thesemodes. This indicates that the seismic waves travel faster and strongly attenuate along polar paths such that the m = 0 singlet remains poorly observed for these modes. The estimation of elastic/anelastic structure for mantle sensitive modes is also equally important, and I am currently working on this.

In the autoregression process, the amplitude spectra for a normal mode is computed at every staion. Then all the good quality spectra are collapsed to obtain a set of spectra known as receiver strips. In this procedure, the receiver location information represented by Spherical harmonics is removed. The example receiver strips are shown in Figures below.

The hierarchical Bayesian inversion results for the amplitude spectra shown above are shown in Figure 3. The Bayesian information criterion (BIC) and Deviance Information Criterion (DIC) indicate that the data for 11S4 mode do not require the anelastic structure in the inversion, but the 13S1 mode requires both elastic and anelastic structure to explain the observed ampltude spectra reasonably well.

Project 5

Crustal structure beneath Himalaya Nepal

The detailed-crustal velocity structure beneath Nepal Himalayas allows us to better understand the tectonic processes and improve the earthquake location and seismic hazard evaluation for the region. Previous studies suggest an anisotropic Main-Himalayan Thrust (MHT) beneath Nepal based on conversions of direct P-waves coming from different backazimuths and distant earthquakes (teleseismic receiver functions). However, proper quantification of isotropic/anisotropic velocity and dipping of MHT and their uncertainties become challenging due to the trade-off between layer properties. Additionally, there are limited studies on the extensive-crustal structures beneath Nepal. We recently developed a hierarchical Bayesian inversion of both radial and transverse receiver functions and here applied it to data from narrow backazimuth and epicentral distance ranges recorded on more than 30 temporary seismic stations in Nepal. The inversion results for the stations near the southern edge of the Himalayan front (main-frontal thrust) show a strong anisotropic layer ( 35±5%) with high dip ( 20±5°) between a very slow-velocity shallow layer above and a fast-velocity deeper layer below it. The anisotropic layer continues northward with low dip angles (5±2°) beneath the stations south of Kathmandu and high dip angles north of Kathmandu. This anisotropic layer may be due to the shearing motion between the northward moving Indian crust with respect to the overriding Himalayan crust. Our inversion further suggests a very high S-wave velocity and strong anisotropy in the top layer, particularly north of the thrust that separates high Himalaya from lesser Himalaya (main-central thrust). Here, we further focus on the inversion of receiver functions from different backazimuths and short-period surface-wave dispersions extracted from the noise. The surface wave dispersions remove the depth-velocity trade-offs and give strong constraints on the average S-wave velocity while the receiver functions from multiple backazimuths enable to better estimate the anisotropy.
The S-wave velocity model beneath Himalaya Nepal is shown in Figure 1. I am currently working on the joint inversion of S-wave velocity and receiver functions.

Project 6

Bayesian modeling of gravity data

Gravity anomalies are crucial observations to infer the sub-surface density structure and its geometry. However, the inversion of gravity data is a non-linear and can be an underdetermined problem resulting in non-unique solutions (Fig. 1). Additionally, the number of anomalies and their geometries are unknowns in the case of observed data and estimation of rigorous uncertainties is challenging using linearized inversion. In order to address these challenges, we develop a trans-dimensional Bayesian inversion of gravity data in which the number of corners and anomalies also become unknown in the inversion, and unknown geometry and its complexity are naturally determined based on the observed data.

Example inversion of gravity data is shown in Figure below. IN this example, the number of curnors (i.e., co-ordinates) are allowed to change and sampling is performed in three different moves: (1) birth, (2) death, and (3) perturbation. In the birth step, a new co-ordinate is introduced while a randomly selected co-ordinate is deleted in the case of death move. In contrast, parameters including density are perturbed without adding/removing co-ordinates. These moves are accepted or rejected applying the acceptance criterion based on the likelihood ratio.

Project 4

This project analyzes the data collected as part of the telemarketing calls by a Portuguese retail bank with the aim of getting long-term deposits so that bank’s financial assets can be increased by enhancing the business. The target variable for this data was whether the campaign call was successful or not. Features from the phone call were combined with the data from central bank of Portuguese Republic statistical web site. After combining all the data, there were 150 features in total. The data have been previously preprocessed and contain no errors. After careful selection features, this dataset has only 20 features. As these data were already processed, I will focus on the statistical analysis and modeling. Here, I apply three methods (logistic regression, decision tree, and neural network) and compare results in terms of their predictability. After careful consideration of feature selection and balancing the data, all the models perform well in predicting outcome for both trained and test data. The comparative analysis of both confusion matrix and ROC curve indicates that neural network performs the best among all the methods.

The dataset I am using in this project was downloaded from the University of California Irvine (UCI) website. The data were collected for 5 years from 2008 to 2013. To collect these data, customers were contacted by bank agents via fixed line or mobile. Alternatively, if the client calls the bank for any other reasons, he/she was asked to subscribe the deposit. This dataset has 20 features and 41188 records. These data include four classes of telemarketing features: (1) bank client data with 7 features (age, job, marital, education, default, housing, and loan), (2) contact related data with 4 features (contact, month, day_of_week, duration), (3) social and economic context with 5 features (emp.var.rate, cons.price.idx, cons.conf.idx, euribor3m, nr.employed), and (4) others with 4 features (campaign, pdays, previous, poutcome). More detailed explanation of these features can be found on both Jupyter notebook and UCI website. As this is a classification problem, the target variable (y) for this problem has two options: whether a client subscribes term deposit (yes or no). Figure 1 shows the bar and pie chart plots for the target variable and illustrates that only ~11% of the calls were subscribed for the deposit indicating highly unbalanced data. Therefore, any prediction made from this data can be biased towards unsuccessful outcome. I checked through all the data and found no errors in the data (both features and responses). But there are missing values reported as unknowns. I replaced missing values by mean and mode for numerical variables and string variables, respectively. I also found outliers except in the case of four features (emp.var.rate, cons.price.idx, euribor3m, and nr.employed). I replaced outliers by the threshold values. See Section 4 in notebook in github for more details.

In this project, I applied three classification methods (logistic regression, decision tree, and neural network) with and without careful processing of data. Logistic regression models the probability of a discrete outcome from the given features. Here, I applied the most common logistic regression that gives binary outcomes (“yes” or “no”). In contrast, decision tree creates branching structures with a set of rules to distinguish values in a hierarchical form. This maybe the simplest approach to understanding, but there is a high chance of overfitting if we do not tune the hyperparameters. I then applied multilayer perceptron neural network with one hidden layer. Neural network with no hidden layers represents the logistic regression. The neural networks are good for learning complex nonlinear problems, particularly for problems with large number of features.
To estimate the model performance for all these models, I computed confusion matrix with accuracy, precision, recall, and F1-score. I also computed Receiver Operating Characteristics (ROC) curves and area under curve (AUC) values. These metrics tell us how correct and precise model predictions are by comparing it against true evaluations. For more details, I refer to the Jupyter notebook (Section 6.1.2).

I explored the data using descriptive and exploratory data analysis (see Section 2-4 in notebook). As mentioned in the data, duration is an important variable as it is directly linked to the outcome. If the call duration is 0, the assumption is that the client does not sign up for bank deposit. As a result, duration has a large uncertainty (challenging to trust) with longest call being 4918 s (~80 minutes). Additionally, box plot indicates that there are many outliers for duration resulting a wider uncertainty. I also find that in average ~10 minutes (553 sec) long call was enough for successful campaign, but it still has high uncertainty, probably due to outliers. Similarly, in average 2 calls were enough for a successful campaign.

Similarly, standard deviation for age indicates that the campaign was intended to a wider age group (17 to 98 years). The average age of all the subscribed clients is ~40 years while the average age of subscribed unmarried clients was 31 years. Additionally, the age between 31 to 50 were most likely to subscribe for the deposit. The multivariate analysis indicates that the longest call was made to a 33-year old unmarried technician and has proessional.course degree. Number of days past from the last contact (pdays) has very high mean value and uncertainty indicating that most clients were not contacted before as that value was set to 999 for those with no contacts before. Comparatively, there are many customers who work as admin, are married, have university degree, have no default, have housing, have no loans, and were contacted on cellular phone during the month of May. The Consumer price index (cons.price.idx) has very low uncertainty indicating very consistent index between the clients selected. Maybe that was one of the criteria on the selection of clients for calls. The histograms for categorical variables indicate that most of the clients (~25%) work as admin.     Research Interests

    Detection of high-resolution earthquakes
  • Detect earthquakes using STA/LTA followed by matched filter approach based on cross-correlation of compressional (P) and shear (S) waves
  • Locate earthquakes and invert for earthquake source properties using traditional approaches and a probabilistic Bayesian inference to have better understanding of parameter values and their uncertainties resulting from measurement errors (e.g., errors in the measurement process) and model errors (e.g., earth structure) which are essential for seismic hazards

    • Crustal and lithospheric structure

  • Receiver functions (RFs), surface wave dispersions (SWDs) signal processing from earthquakes and ambient noise with their analysis
  • Joint inversion of RFs and dispersions extracted from dense small arrays for shallow crustal structure
  • Joint inversion of RFs and SWDs to study the structure of Earth lithosphere
  • Joint Bayesian inversion of RFs and SWDs to address the problem of non-uniqueness and parameter uncertainties
  • Imaging the lithospheric structure using both local and regional earthquake travel-time residuals
  • Crustal attenuation using strong ground motion data
  • Trans-dimensional Bayesian inversion of gravity data

  • Seismic migration of short period waveforms to study the small-scale heterogeneities in the lowermost mantle
  • Study of small-scale structures by applying multi-disciplinary approach
